Agents act. Sometimes they shouldn't.

The interesting agents aren't the ones that answer questions — they're the ones that do things. And the moment an agent can take an action in the real world, there's a gap between what it intends and what it should be allowed to do. Today, almost nothing sits in that gap.

Irreversible by default

A payment, a deletion, a deploy, an outbound email. Once it runs, "undo" usually isn't an option.

Hard-coded rules don't scale

You can't write an if statement for every action an agent might take across every tool it can call.

All-or-nothing autonomy

Most setups either trust the agent fully or babysit every step. Neither is workable in production.

How it works

Middleware between intent and execution.

Kelhe is a thin call your agent makes right before it executes a tool. It returns a risk score and a decision. Your agent does the rest.

01

Agent proposes

Before executing, the agent sends Kelhe the action it's about to take, plus context — who, what, how much, on whose behalf.

→
02

Kelhe scores it

The engine evaluates the action against risk signals — impact, reversibility, value, sensitivity, anomaly — and returns a single score.

→
03

A decision comes back

Proceed, hold for a human, or deny — with a reason your agent (and your audit log) can act on.

Three outcomes. One contract.

Every action resolves to exactly one of these.

✓ PROCEED

Low risk

The action is safe and routine. Kelhe gets out of the way and the agent runs it immediately.

⏸ HOLD

Needs a human

Ambiguous or high-stakes. The action is paused and routed to a person for approval before anything happens.

✕ DENY

Out of bounds

The action crosses a hard line. Kelhe blocks it and returns a reason instead of an apology after the fact.
